WORLD HAPPINESS ANALYSIS

By Govind Chadha

Happiness is a feeling everyone seeks, acting as a friend that makes life joyful. It is not just about personal joy but also about the health and well-being of communities around the world.

People find happiness in various ways. Some find it in relationships, others in their jobs, and some in the things they own. Each person has unique things that bring them happiness and peace.

This report aims to understand happiness in different parts of the world. We will look at the Happiness Score in various countries, focusing on the top 10 happiest places, and study the different levels of happiness in various areas.

Our main goal is to see if money, specifically GDP per capita, affects how happy people are in different countries. We will also explore whether the freedom to make choices impacts a country's overall happiness. This report will show how money and freedom are spread out in each country as well.

A key facet of our exploration will be to determine if a nation's economy, specifically GDP per capita, influences its collective happiness. Moreover, we'll investigate if there's a correlation between a country's happiness and the freedom to make choices by its residents. The spatial distribution of each nation's GDP per capita and freedom to make choices will also be highlighted in this visualization.

Top 10 Happiest Countries In The World

Finland is hailed as the happiest place on earth thanks to its extensive welfare benefits, low levels of corruption, and a well-functioning democracy.

Denmark boasts one of the most generous retirement systems in the world. Its short working hours play a crucial role in it being the second happiest country globally.

Norway offers tuition-free quality education to its citizens. Furthermore, Norwegians possess a robust network of family ties and a profound sense of identity, factors that significantly contribute to the nation's happiness.

Life Expectancy and Happiness Analysis Based On Region

There's a noticeable upward trend suggesting that countries with a higher Life Expectancy tend to have higher happiness scores. This indicates a positive correlation between Health Quality and the perceived happiness of the population.

Regions such as Western Europe and North America showcase countries with both high quality of life and high happiness scores. Conversely, regions like Sub-Saharan Africa and Southern Asia have countries that lean towards the lower end of both metrics.

Countries like Qatar and Hong Kong have high health quality but don't align perfectly with the trend, suggesting that economic Health isn't the sole determinant of happiness. On the other hand, countries like India have a massive population and a considerable Health score, but the happiness score isn't as high as some Western countries.

The size of each circle represents the population of the respective country. This offers a layered understanding where countries with larger circles have a higher population. For example, countries with populations exceeding 500M are represented with the largest circles.

Despite similarities in Health, countries like Honduras and Mexico have different happiness scores. This highlights that while economy plays a role, cultural, political, and other factors can influence happiness.
